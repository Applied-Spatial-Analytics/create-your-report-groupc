6  Discussion

6.1 Interpretation of the main findings

The aim of this project was to analyse how green-space configuration relates to pluvial flood-related surface water change in Delft and Xi’an. The results show that there is a measurable relationship between the green-space metrics and the FWEI-based indicators, but this relationship is not straightforward.

In both cities, higher green coverage, larger green patches, and more contiguous green areas were generally associated with higher FWEI change or higher flood share. This is different from the simple expectation that more green space should always reduce flood-related surface water. The findings therefore need to be interpreted as spatial associations, not as direct causal effects.

One possible explanation is that green spaces are often located in areas where surface water can accumulate and remain visible after rainfall. Parks, fields, open spaces, and low-lying green areas may temporarily store water. In a remote-sensing-based index such as FWEI, this can appear as increased surface water. In this sense, higher FWEI values in green areas do not necessarily mean that green spaces are performing badly. They may indicate that water is being stored or retained in open areas rather than moving immediately into built-up spaces.

Another possible explanation is related to detectability. Surface water may be easier to detect in open green areas than in dense urban areas. In built-up areas, water can be hidden by buildings, shadows, narrow streets, roofs, or drainage infrastructure. This means that the FWEI signal may capture surface water more clearly in open landscapes than in compact urban fabric. Therefore, the positive relationship between green metrics and FWEI-based indicators may partly reflect how remote sensing observes water, rather than the actual hydrological effect of green space.

6.2 Comparison between Delft and Xi’an

A useful outcome of the project is that the same workflow could be applied to both Delft and Xi’an. The two cities differ strongly in size, urban structure, climate context, and data availability. Despite these differences, the grid-based method produced comparable outputs for both cases. This supports the use of a shared spatial framework for cross-city green infrastructure analysis, which is important because differences in data and spatial units can make comparisons difficult (Meerow 2019).

The typology results were especially useful for comparison. Both cities showed four similar green-space configurations: highly green and contiguous areas, moderately green connected areas, sparse green fragmented areas, and low-green dispersed areas. This suggests that similar spatial categories can be identified across different urban contexts when the same metrics and spatial units are used.

However, the strength of the relationships differed between the cities. Delft showed the strongest correlation between mean patch area and flood share, while Xi’an showed the strongest correlation between green percentage and flood share. Xi’an also showed a negative relationship between mean distance to green and flood share, suggesting that FWEI-derived surface water increase was closer to green space. In Delft, this relationship was positive. This difference may be caused by differences in urban form, water networks, land-cover structure, elevation, or the timing and quality of the FWEI imagery.

6.3 Meaning of the typology results

The typology analysis showed that the highly green and contiguous typology had the highest flood-related values in both cities. This is one of the most important findings of the project. It shows that the relationship between green infrastructure and surface water is not only about how much green space is present, but also where that green space is located and how water appears in the landscape.

In planning terms, highly green and contiguous areas may function as water storage spaces during or after rainfall. If water is detected in these areas, it may not always indicate a negative flood outcome. It may also indicate that open spaces are retaining water that would otherwise contribute to runoff elsewhere. This connects to the wider idea that green infrastructure can provide stormwater management benefits, but that these benefits depend on spatial context and planning goals (Brom et al. 2023).

The typology approach also makes the results easier to interpret than the correlation analysis alone. Correlations show relationships between individual variables, but typologies show combinations of spatial characteristics. For example, Typology 2 is not only high in green percentage, but also high in patch area and contiguity. This makes it possible to discuss green-space patterns as spatial types rather than isolated metrics.

6.4 Limitations

There are several limitations in this project. The first limitation is that FWEI is used as a proxy for flood-related surface water change. It does not measure flood depth, water volume, or direct flood damage. The variable flood_share is based on a threshold applied to FWEI change, so it should be interpreted as an FWEI-derived surface-water increase indicator rather than a confirmed flood map.

The second limitation is that equivalent high-resolution pluvial flood-risk data were not available for both cities. Delft had more local flood-related data available, while Xi’an did not have the same type of dataset. Because of this, the project used FWEI for both cities to keep the comparison consistent. This improves comparability, but it also means that the analysis depends strongly on the quality and timing of the satellite imagery.

The third limitation is that the analysis does not include all factors that influence pluvial flooding. Flood-related surface water change is affected by rainfall intensity, drainage capacity, soil conditions, elevation, slope, sewer systems, surface materials, and local water management. This project mainly focuses on green-space configuration, so the results cannot fully explain why flooding occurs in specific locations.

The fourth limitation is related to the grid-based method. A 100 m by 100 m grid makes comparison easier, but it also simplifies the urban landscape. Some small-scale features may be lost or averaged within grid cells. A different grid size could produce slightly different results. Therefore, the results should be understood as scale-dependent.

The fifth limitation is that the K-means typologies depend on the selected input variables and the chosen number of clusters. In this project, four clusters were used because they produced interpretable green-space types. However, a different number of clusters could lead to a different typology structure.

6.5 Reflection on the method

The method worked well for creating a comparable spatial workflow. The use of binary green rasters, 100 m grid cells, patch metrics, FWEI change, and K-means clustering allowed the same analysis to be applied to both Delft and Xi’an. This is useful for comparative spatial analysis because it avoids relying on city-specific administrative units or incompatible flood datasets.

The decision to focus on patch metrics was also important. Since the green rasters are binary, patch metrics are more suitable than broad class metrics for describing green-space configuration. Metrics such as patch area, contiguity, and nearest-neighbour distance provide interpretable information about the size, internal connection, and isolation of green patches.

At the same time, the results show that spatial metrics alone are not enough to explain flood-related processes. The positive relationships between green-space metrics and FWEI-based indicators show that interpretation must include urban context, data limitations, and remote-sensing detectability. This supports the idea that green infrastructure planning requires spatial decision support, but also careful interpretation of the indicators being used (Sarabi et al. 2022).

6.6 Implications for green infrastructure planning

The findings suggest that planners should be careful when interpreting green-space quantity as a simple solution to pluvial flooding. More green space is not automatically associated with lower FWEI-derived surface water change in this analysis. Instead, green spaces may also be places where water is stored, retained, or more easily detected after rainfall.

This does not reduce the importance of green infrastructure. Rather, it shows that green infrastructure should be evaluated based on its spatial role within the wider urban system. Large contiguous green spaces may provide storage capacity, cooling, recreation, and ecological benefits, but their relationship with flood indicators depends on topography, drainage, and surrounding built-up areas.

For future planning, the typology approach could be useful for identifying areas where green infrastructure has different potential roles. Highly green and contiguous areas may be important for water retention. Low-green dispersed areas may be priorities for additional greening. Sparse green fragmented areas may require different interventions, such as small-scale infiltration measures, street trees, or permeable surfaces.

6.7 Future research

Future research could improve the analysis by including additional explanatory variables. Elevation, slope, flow accumulation, soil type, impervious surface percentage, drainage infrastructure, and rainfall intensity would help explain why certain areas show more FWEI-derived surface water increase. Including these variables could make it easier to separate the effect of green-space configuration from other spatial factors.

The analysis could also be improved by testing different grid sizes. Comparing 50 m, 100 m, and 200 m grids would show how sensitive the results are to spatial scale. Another improvement would be to test different FWEI thresholds for the flood_share variable. This would show whether the results are stable or whether they depend strongly on the chosen threshold.

Finally, future work could compare the FWEI-derived results with observed flood reports, local flood maps, or hydrological modelling outputs. This would help validate whether the FWEI signal corresponds to actual flood conditions.
