2  Preliminary maps and research

2.1 Study area selection

The study areas were selected to allow a consistent comparison between Delft and Xi’an. Because the two cities are very different in size, it would not be meaningful to compare their full administrative boundaries directly. Delft is relatively compact, while Xi’an is a much larger metropolitan area. A full-city comparison would therefore create an imbalance in scale, data volume, and spatial heterogeneity.

To reduce this problem, both cities were analysed using a 10 km by 10 km study area. For Delft, this extent covers the city and its immediate surroundings. For Xi’an, the same-sized extent was placed over a central urban area that contains a mixture of dense built-up land, green spaces, roads, and open areas. This creates a more balanced comparison because both cases are analysed using the same spatial extent.

A 100 m by 100 m grid was then created for each city. This grid is the main spatial unit of analysis. All green-space metrics and flood-related indicators were calculated per grid cell. The grid-based approach makes it possible to compare different datasets with different raster resolutions in a common spatial framework. It also allows the results to be mapped, compared, and clustered into spatial typologies.

2.2 Delft and Xi’an as comparison cases

Delft and Xi’an were chosen because they represent contrasting urban and environmental contexts. Delft is a smaller Dutch city located in a low-lying delta landscape. It contains a dense network of canals, water bodies, streets, buildings, and urban green spaces. Because of its low elevation and highly managed water system, Delft is a relevant case for studying flood-related urban water issues.

Xi’an is much larger and has a different urban structure. The selected Xi’an study area represents a dense urban environment with large built-up areas, major roads, and green spaces distributed across the city. Compared with Delft, Xi’an has a different climate context, different land-cover patterns, and a different scale of urban development. This contrast makes it useful for testing whether a shared spatial method can identify comparable green-space patterns in very different cities.

The comparison is therefore not based on the assumption that Delft and Xi’an are similar cities. Instead, the project uses them as contrasting cases. The purpose is to examine whether a common spatial workflow can be applied to both cities and whether similar green-space typologies can be identified despite their differences.

2.3 Natural and flood-related context

The flood-related focus of this project is pluvial flooding. Pluvial flooding is connected to intense rainfall and surface water accumulation within the urban area. This is different from coastal flooding or river flooding, which are mainly driven by sea levels, storm surges, or river discharge. A pluvial focus is useful for this project because rainfall-driven surface water accumulation can occur in both Delft and Xi’an, even though their wider geographical settings are different.

For Delft, local flood-related data were available, but an equivalent high-resolution pluvial flood-risk dataset was not available for Xi’an. To keep the comparison consistent, the main flood-related indicator in the project is therefore based on FWEI. For both cities, FWEI layers before and after a rainfall or flood-related event were used. The difference between the after and before layers was calculated as an indicator of surface water change.

This approach does not measure flood depth directly. Instead, it provides a remote-sensing-based proxy for areas where surface water increased between the two moments. For this reason, the project uses careful wording such as “flood-related surface water change” rather than treating the FWEI output as a direct flood-depth map.

2.4 Built environment and land-cover context

The built environment is important for this project because impervious surfaces influence how rainfall moves through cities. Dense built-up areas often contain more hard surfaces and fewer infiltration opportunities. Green spaces can interrupt this pattern by providing more permeable surfaces, but their effect may depend on how they are arranged.

The analysis therefore uses binary green-space rasters for both Delft and Xi’an. In these rasters, green areas are represented as value 1 and non-green areas as value 0. The Delft green-space raster was created from local land-cover processing and was originally very high resolution. The Xi’an green-space raster was derived from a high-resolution urban green-space dataset. To make the metric calculation more computationally manageable and comparable, both green rasters were aggregated to approximately 5 m resolution before landscape metrics were calculated.

Initial visual inspection showed that both cities contain a mixture of highly green cells, moderately green cells, low-green cells, and cells with almost no green space. This supported the decision to move beyond simple green coverage and include landscape metrics that describe the configuration of green patches.

2.5 First data exploration

The first data exploration confirmed that the grid-based approach could be used for both cities. The final workflow successfully calculated green-space metrics for 10,000 grid cells in Delft and 10,000 grid cells in Xi’an. For each grid cell, the analysis calculated green coverage, patch area, radius of gyration, contiguity, nearest-neighbour distance, FWEI change, flood share, and distance from FWEI-derived flooded pixels to the nearest green pixel.

The first outputs also showed that the method produces meaningful spatial variation. Some grid cells contain large and contiguous green patches, while others contain only small, fragmented, or almost absent green areas. The FWEI-based variables also vary spatially, allowing comparison between green-space configuration and flood-related surface water change.

The preliminary analysis also showed that the relationship is not as simple as “more green space means less flood-related water”. In the current outputs, some greener cells also show higher FWEI change or flood share. This suggests that the results need to be interpreted carefully. Green spaces may be located in low-lying, open, or wetter areas where surface water is more visible in remote-sensing data. Therefore, the analysis should be understood as an exploration of spatial association rather than proof of direct causality.

The preliminary findings support the need for a typology-based approach. Instead of only analysing individual correlations, the project groups grid cells into green-space typologies. These typologies help describe recurring spatial patterns, such as highly green contiguous areas, moderately green connected areas, low-green dispersed areas, and sparse fragmented areas. These groups can then be compared with the FWEI-based flood-related indicators.
